The Evidence

The IV route is the highest-harm vector.
Most systems provide the least support for it.

54%

Of potentially harmful medication errors in hospital settings involve the intravenous route — disproportionate to IV's share of total medication administrations, and consistent across healthcare systems studied.

Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP); AHRQ Patient Safety Network — Medication Error Reporting
3×

IV route errors are approximately three times more likely to result in serious patient harm compared to oral route errors. Rapid onset, systemic delivery, and irreversibility remove the interception window available in other routes.

AHRQ Patient Safety Network; ISMP Comparative Route Harm Analysis; American Journal of Health-System Pharmacy
400K+

Preventable adverse drug events occur annually in U.S. hospitals. IV administration errors contribute disproportionately to fatal outcomes — concentrated in complex, high-acuity patients with the most lines and the most medications.

Institute of Medicine, Preventing Medication Errors (2006); AHRQ Medication Safety Program
Published figures reflect what reaches voluntary reporting systems and administrative databases. Subject-matter consensus holds that actual IV error frequency is an order of magnitude higher. The visible number is the floor, not the ceiling. LineMap was designed for what is counted and for what never gets reported.
The Gap

The system didn't fail.
It never existed.

Every clinical environment has solved IV line management the same way: memory, phone calls, and peer checks in real time under maximum cognitive load. None of these scale. None of these leave a record.

01

Compatibility is solved by recall and consultation

Clinicians managing active infusions rely on fragmented reference apps, institutional memory, and peer consultation — in real time, on already-loaded cognitive bandwidth. These methods do not scale and do not create accountability trails.

02

Lumen capacity is invisible until access fails

IV access constraints are communicated verbally and reconciled at the bedside. No centralized, real-time view of line architecture exists. Decisions are made without situational awareness of what each lumen is carrying.

03

The highest-complexity patients receive the least support

Critically ill patients have the most IV medications, the most central lines, and the highest consequence for error. They are also the patients for whom real-time infusion decision support is most absent.

04

Documentation records events. It does not prevent them.

EHR infusion documentation is retrospective, structured for billing, and does not operate in the decision interval before administration. The gap between clinical decision and any safety check is unmonitored.

What LineMap Is

Decision support at the moment of infusion. Not before. Not after.

LineMap is not documentation. It is not a reference lookup. It is not an EHR replacement. It is lumen-aware, compatibility-aware, real-time cognitive support — at the exact interval where IV decisions happen and where current systems are silent.

Real-Time Compatibility

Instant Y-site and co-administration compatibility checks at the point of decision — surfaced without leaving clinical workflow.

Lumen Architecture View

Full visibility into IV access sites, lumen assignments, current infusions, and capacity — organized by patient, updated in real time.

Structured Safety Alerts

Non-intrusive alerts at the point of incompatibility or access conflict. Designed to reduce cognitive load, not amplify alarm fatigue.

Dwell Time Monitoring

Automated dwell tracking with evidence-based threshold alerts — flagging access past safe windows before complications develop.

Unit-Wide Patient Access Board

Real-time view across all active lines, access status, and risk flags for the entire unit — for charge nurses and clinical leads.

EHR Integration Ready

Architecture designed for downstream HL7 FHIR connectivity. Operational as a standalone layer today. Integration-ready for tomorrow.
